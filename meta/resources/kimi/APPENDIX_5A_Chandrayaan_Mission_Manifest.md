# Appendix 5A: Indian Chandrayaan Programme Mission Manifest

**Programme:** Chandrayaan (2008–present)  
**Document Type:** Mission Manifest  
**Date Compiled:** 2026-04-04  
**Source Priority:** ISRO official mission pages, ISRO annual reports, Chandrayaan-2 Failure Analysis Committee findings

---

## Complete Mission Table

| Mission | Launch Date | Launch Vehicle | Components | Primary Objective | Outcome | Key Notes |
|---------|-------------|----------------|------------|-------------------|---------|-----------|
| Chandrayaan-1 | 22 Oct 2008 | PSLV-XL C11 | Orbiter (523 kg) + Moon Impact Probe (35 kg) | Lunar orbital survey; mineral mapping; water detection | Success (partial) | Operated 312 days (planned 2 years); discovered water molecules on lunar surface; MIP impacted Moon 14 Nov 2008 [Source: ISRO] |
| Chandrayaan-2 | 22 Jul 2019 | GSLV Mk III M1 | Orbiter (2,379 kg) + Vikram Lander (1,471 kg) + Pragyan Rover (27 kg) | First southern polar landing; surface exploration | Partial success | Orbiter successful (7-year mission); Vikram lander crashed during descent at ~2.1 km altitude; orbiter continues to operate [Source: ISRO] |
| Chandrayaan-3 | 14 Jul 2023 | GSLV Mk III M4 | Vikram Lander (1,749 kg) + Pragyan Rover (26 kg) | Southern polar soft landing; surface exploration | Success | First soft landing in lunar south polar region (69.37°S); mission cost ~$75 million USD; operated 14 days (1 lunar day) [Source: ISRO] |
| Chandrayaan-4 | 2027–2028 (planned) | LVM3 / Multiple | Sample return stack | Lunar sample return | Planned | Four-module architecture: orbiter, lander, ascender, returner [Source: ISRO] |
| LUPEX | 2028–2029 (planned) | H3 (JAXA) / LVM3 | Lander + Rover (with JAXA) | Lunar polar exploration; water ice detection | Planned | JAXA-ISRO joint mission; targeting permanently shadowed regions [Source: ISRO/JAXA] |

## Programme Cost Summary

| Mission | Cost (USD) | Notes |
|---------|------------|-------|
| Chandrayaan-1 | ~$56 million | Including international payloads |
| Chandrayaan-2 | ~$142 million | Including orbiter, lander, rover |
| Chandrayaan-3 | ~$75 million | Lander + rover only (orbiter from Chandrayaan-2) |
| **Total Programme (2008–2023)** | **~$273 million** | Three missions |

**Comparison:** Chandrayaan-3's $75 million cost is approximately 10% of a typical NASA Discovery-class mission and demonstrates ISRO's frugal engineering approach [Source: ISRO, PNAS].

## Chandrayaan-1 Mission Details

### Orbiter Specifications

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Launch Mass** | 523 kg |
| **Dry Mass** | 440 kg |
| **Power** | 750 W solar |
| **Orbit** | 100 km polar circular |
| **Mission Duration** | 312 days (contact lost 28 Aug 2009) |
| **Planned Duration** | 2 years |

### Instruments

| Instrument | Provider | Purpose |
|------------|----------|---------|
| **TMC** | ISRO | Terrain Mapping Camera (5m resolution) |
| **HySI** | ISRO | Hyper Spectral Imager |
| **LLRI** | ISRO | Lunar Laser Ranging Instrument |
| **HEX** | ISRO | High Energy X-ray Spectrometer |
| **M3** | NASA | Moon Mineralogy Mapper |
| **SIR-2** | ESA | Near-Infrared Spectrometer |
| **RADOM** | Bulgaria | Radiation Dose Monitor |
| **Mini-SAR** | NASA | Synthetic Aperture Radar |

### Moon Impact Probe (MIP)

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Mass** | 35 kg |
| **Release Date** | 14 Nov 2008 |
| **Impact Location** | Shackleton crater vicinity (89.76°S) |
| **Instruments** | Radar altimeter, video imaging system, mass spectrometer |

**Key Discovery:** The Moon Mineralogy Mapper (NASA/M3) on Chandrayaan-1 provided definitive evidence of water molecules (OH and H2O) on the lunar surface, particularly at polar regions. This discovery transformed understanding of the Moon and influenced subsequent mission planning worldwide [Source: ISRO, NASA].

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Mass** | 27 kg |
| **Power** | 50 W solar |
| **Mobility** | 6 wheels |

**Failure Analysis:** The Vikram lander experienced a navigation/guidance anomaly during the rough braking phase. Software-induced trajectory deviation caused the lander to deviate from planned path. The failure occurred at approximately 2.1 km altitude during powered descent [Source: ISRO Failure Analysis Committee].

## Chandrayaan-3 Mission Details

### Vikram Lander (Redesigned)

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Mass** | 1,749 kg (increased from 1,471 kg) |
| **Power** | Increased solar panel area |
| **Landing Site** | 69.37°S, 32.35°E |
| **Landing Date** | 23 Aug 2023 |

### Key Design Changes from Chandrayaan-2

| Aspect | Chandrayaan-2 | Chandrayaan-3 |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| **Design Philosophy** | Success-path optimised | Failure-based design |
| **Landing Zone** | 500 m × 500 m | 4 km × 2.5 km |
| **Fuel Margin** | Standard | Additional fuel for alternate sites |
| **Solar Panels** | Fixed orientation | Increased area; orientation-independent |
| **Navigation Sensors** | Standard suite | Added Laser Doppler Velocimeter |
| **Team Continuity** | — | Same team retained; no-blame mandate |
| **Structural Strength** | Standard | Enhanced for higher touchdown loads |
| **Instrument Redundancy** | Limited | Increased |

### Pragyan Rover (Chandrayaan-3)

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **Mass** | 26 kg |
| **Power** | 50 W solar |
| **Mobility** | 6 wheels |
| **Instruments** | APXS, LIBS |
| **Distance Travelled** | ~100 m |
| **Operating Duration** | 14 days (1 lunar day) |

### Onboard Instruments

| Instrument | Full Name | Purpose |
|------------|-----------|---------|
| **APXS** | Alpha Particle X-ray Spectrometer | Elemental composition |
| **LIBS** | Laser-Induced Breakdown Spectroscope | Surface composition |
| **ChaSTE** | Chandra's Surface Thermophysical Experiment | Thermal properties |
| **ILSA** | Instrument for Lunar Seismic Activity | Seismic monitoring |
| **RAMBHA-LP** | Radio Anatomy of Moon Bound Hypersensitive Atmosphere | Plasma environment |

**Historic Achievement:** Chandrayaan-3 made India the fourth country to achieve lunar soft landing (after USSR, USA, China) and the first to land in the lunar south polar region. The landing site at 69.37°S is the southernmost successful landing to date [Source: ISRO].
