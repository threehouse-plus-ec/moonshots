# Chinese Chang’e Programme – Task Card 4 Appendices
This appendix compiles mission and systems data for China’s Chang’e lunar exploration programme (Chinese Lunar Exploration Program, CLEP), including its four robotic phases, associated relay satellites, and links to future International Lunar Research Station (ILRS) and crewed‑lunar plans.[^1][^2]
## Deliverable 4A – Mission Manifest
### Four‑phase structure of the Chinese Lunar Exploration Program
The CLEP is commonly described as a four‑phase architecture: Phase 1 (orbiters Chang’e 1 and 2), Phase 2 (landers and rovers Chang’e 3 and 4), Phase 3 (sample return Chang’e 5 and 6), and Phase 4 (south‑polar research station precursor missions Chang’e 7 and 8).[^3][^4][^5][^2]

|Phase|Missions|Description|
|-----|--------|-----------|
|Phase 1 – Orbit|Chang’e 1, Chang’e 2|First lunar orbiters to map global topography, composition, and environment, providing datasets and engineering knowledge for later landing and sample‑return missions.[^3][^6][^7]|
|Phase 2 – Landing and roving|Chang’e 3 (Yutu), Chang’e 4 (Yutu‑2)|Near‑side and far‑side soft landings with rovers, enabling in situ geology, environment, and technology demonstrations on the lunar surface.[^8][^9][^10][^11]|
|Phase 3 – Sample return|Chang’e 5, Chang’e 6|Near‑side (Mons Rümker region) and far‑side (South Pole–Aitken basin) robotic sample‑return missions, returning kilogram‑scale quantities of regolith to Earth.[^1][^5][^12][^13][^14]|
|Phase 4 – Research station precursors|Chang’e 7, Chang’e 8|Multi‑element missions to the south pole with orbiters, landers, rovers, and hoppers to survey volatiles and test in‑situ resource utilisation and infrastructure technologies as precursors to the ILRS.[^15][^16][^17][^2]|

### Spacecraft types per phase
#### Phase 1: Orbiters (Chang’e 1 and 2)

- **Chang’e 1:** Selenocentric polar‑orbiting spacecraft with microwave radiometer, laser altimeter, CCD stereo camera, and gamma‑ray/X‑ray spectrometers; mass ≈2,350 kg.[^3][^6]
- **Chang’e 2:** Improved orbiter with higher‑resolution imaging (to ≈7 m), lower‑altitude mapping, and extended mission to Sun–Earth L2 and asteroid Toutatis.[^1][^22]

Functions included global DEM generation, mineralogical mapping, regolith thickness estimation, and stereo imaging for landing‑site selection.[^7][^2]

#### Phase 2: Landers and rovers (Chang’e 3, Chang’e 4)

- **Lander:** LLV with throttleable main engine, four landing legs, hazard‑avoidance sensors, and science payloads such as cameras, a UV telescope (CE‑3), and surface instruments.[^8][^23]
- **Rovers (Yutu, Yutu‑2):** ~1.5 m long, ~1.0 m wide, ~1.0 m high; mass ≈120–140 kg; powered by solar arrays with RHUs for night survival; payload includes panorama cameras, ground‑penetrating radar, alpha‑particle X‑ray spectrometer, and infrared imaging spectrometer.[^28][^24][^23]

Chang’e 4 architecture reuses modified CE‑3 designs but adds dependency on Queqiao relay due to far‑side operations.[^10][^27][^28]

#### Phase 3: Sample‑return (Chang’e 5/6)

Chang’e 5/6 employ a four‑component architecture:

- **Orbiter/returner:** Provides lunar orbit insertion, rendezvous, docking, and Earth‑return trajectory; carries entry capsule.[^35][^5][^31]
- **Lander:** Soft‑lands with drilling rig and scoop to collect surface and subsurface regolith; mass ≈3,200 kg in CE‑5 configuration.[^5][^12]
- **Ascender:** Launches from lander deck with sample canister; mass ≈700 kg; performs lunar‑orbit rendezvous and sample transfer.[^35][^5]
- **Return capsule:** Atmospheric re‑entry vehicle that separates from orbiter near Earth and lands by parachute in Inner Mongolia.[^13][^31]

Chang’e 5 returned 1,731 g of near‑side Mare basalts; Chang’e 6 returned 1,935.3 g of far‑side materials, using similar architecture with adapted landing and communications.[^12][^14][^31][^13]

#### Phase 4: South‑polar research station precursors (Chang’e 7 and 8)

- **Chang’e 7 stack:** Comprised of orbiter, lander, rover, and a mini‑hopper designed to “jump” into shadowed craters for in situ volatile detection.[^15][^2][^39]
- **Chang’e 8 stack:** Lander and rover plus ISRU payloads and small ecosystem experiment to test regolith melting, brick fabrication, and closed biological systems.[^16][^2][^17]

These missions are intended to map resources, test technologies, and validate operations needed for ILRS construction in the 2030s.[^2][^17]
### Queqiao relay architecture
The Queqiao and Queqiao‑2 relay satellites form a specialised communications infrastructure for far‑side and polar Chang’e missions.[^37][^25][^26]

|Satellite|Orbit and role|Key characteristics|
|---------|--------------|-------------------|
|Queqiao|Earth–Moon L2 halo orbit ≈65,000 km beyond Moon; relays between far‑side lander/rover and Earth.|Uses halo orbit where gravity and orbital motion balance, enabling continuous line‑of‑sight to far side and Earth; orbit period ≈14 days with station‑keeping every ≈7 days.[^26][^27]|
|Queqiao‑2|Circumlunar orbit designed to support Chang’e‑6/7/8 and future polar/far‑side operations.|Launched 2024 on Long March 8; provides enhanced relay capacity and flexibility for Phase 4 missions and ILRS precursors.[^37][^38]|
### Four‑phase programme design and backup–primary reuse pattern
China’s lunar programme employs a systematic pattern in which each phase is implemented with a primary spacecraft and a near‑identical backup that can be repurposed for more ambitious follow‑on missions.[^1][^40][^5][^2]

- Phase 1: Chang’e 1 primary orbiter; Chang’e 2 as backup, later used for higher‑resolution mapping, L2 operations, and asteroid flyby.[^7][^22][^1]
- Phase 2: Chang’e 3 primary near‑side lander/rover; Chang’e 4, originally backup, repurposed as far‑side lander/rover using Queqiao relay.[^28][^10][^2]
- Phase 3: Chang’e 5 primary near‑side sample return; Chang’e 6, originally backup, repurposed as far‑side sample‑return mission.[^5][^13][^14]
- Phase 4: Chang’e 7 and 8 continue this iterative approach, with CE‑7 focusing on reconnaissance and CE‑8 on resource‑utilisation and construction tests feeding into ILRS design.[^2][^17]
### International Lunar Research Station (ILRS)
The ILRS is a planned joint China–Russia (and partners) lunar research infrastructure in the south‑polar region, implemented in two stages: a basic station by around 2035 and an expanded station by around 2045.[^41][^17][^42]

|Aspect|Planned architecture|
|------|--------------------|
|Location|South‑polar region of the Moon with surface elements and orbital components, plus supporting Earth‑based segment.[^17][^2]|
|Partners|Core cooperation between CNSA and Roscosmos under 2021 MoU; open to additional international partners for instruments and infrastructure.[^41][^42]|
|Phasing|Phase 1: basic station by ~2035 with power, communications, transport, and experimental facilities; Phase 2: expanded, fully functional station by ~2045 including lunar‑orbit station as hub.[^17]|
|Relation to Chang’e missions|Chang’e‑6 provides far‑side samples; Chang’e‑7 maps polar environment and resources; Chang’e‑8 tests ISRU and construction techniques, all feeding into ILRS planning and design.[^17][^2]|
### Crewed lunar‑programme elements
![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b8/Chinese_crewed_lunar_lander_mockup_02_-_NMC.jpg)
Model of the Chinese crewed lunar lander, displayed in 2023.
China’s crewed lunar plans involve a deep‑space crew vehicle (often referred to as *Mengzhou* in some sources) and a dedicated crewed lunar lander (sometimes referred to as *Lanyue*), both to be launched by Long March 10.[^2][^43]

|Element|Role and key parameters|
|-------|----------------------|
|New‑generation crewed spacecraft|Multi‑module vehicle with service module and re‑entry capsule designed for deep‑space missions; capable of carrying crew to lunar orbit and back; mass and configuration sufficient to dock with a lunar lander and/or ILRS orbital elements.[^2]|
|Lunar lander (Lanyue concept)|Two‑stage lander for crewed descent and ascent; mock‑ups displayed publicly show a wide base stage and compact ascent cabin; designed for south‑polar operations and ILRS support.[^2]|
|Long March 10|Human‑rated super‑heavy launcher designed to place ≈27–30 t into lunar transfer orbit, launching crewed spacecraft and lander elements for dual‑launch rendezvous architecture.[^43]|
The crewed architecture is intended to operate in concert with ILRS infrastructure and Chang’e robotic assets, targeting a first Chinese crewed lunar landing around 2030.[^28][^2]
