### CL-LUNA-001 — Fragmented Bureau Structure and Reliability

**Entry ID:** CL-LUNA-001  
**Global ID:** CL-2026-025  
**Selection Mode:** REQUEST  
**Domain:** Programme architecture, systems reliability  
**Claim Type:** H  
**Scope:** Whether the fragmented design bureau structure of the Soviet lunar programme reduced overall system reliability compared to Apollo's integrated approach. "Fragmented" means multiple competing design bureaux with overlapping mandates and no unified systems-engineering authority. "Reliability" is measured by mission success rate. Comparison set: Apollo programme's five-box management structure.  
**Op. Fidelity:** Moderate — mission outcomes are well documented; attributing causation from organisational structure to reliability requires interpretation.

#### Section A — Claim

The fragmented design bureau structure reduced overall system reliability compared to Apollo's integrated approach.

#### Section B — Predictions

If the claim is true, we expect:

1. The Soviet lunar programme had a significantly lower mission success rate than Apollo.
2. The organisational structure involved competing bureaux without unified systems integration authority.
3. Failure modes can be traced to coordination failures, interface problems, or competing design philosophies attributable to bureau fragmentation.
4. Apollo's integrated management structure (five-box model under a single programme office) is associated with higher reliability outcomes.
5. Where the Soviet programme consolidated authority under a single bureau (e.g., Lavochkin for robotic missions post-1965), reliability improved.

*Plausible alternative (G-6):* The low success rate may owe more to the aggressive launch tempo, inadequate ground testing, and immature technology (particularly in the early programme) than to organisational structure per se. Many early failures were launch vehicle failures, not spacecraft coordination problems. Competition between bureaux may have spurred innovation even as it degraded integration.

#### Section C — Constraints

1. The Luna programme launched 48 missions between 1958 and 1976 (including unnumbered and Kosmos-designated failures). Of these, 15 are classified as fully successful, yielding a 31% overall success rate. [Source: Appendix 3A; Siddiqi, *Challenge to Apollo*, NASA SP-2000-4408]
2. Three principal design bureaux competed for lunar programme authority: OKB-1 (Korolev, later TsKBEM under Mishin) held overall crewed programme responsibility and designed the N1 launcher and L3 complex; NPO Lavochkin (Babakin) was assigned robotic lunar missions from 1965; and OKB-52 (Chelomei) proposed competing crewed circumlunar architecture (UR-700/LK-700) and developed the Proton launcher. [Source: Siddiqi; Harvey, *Soviet and Russian Lunar Exploration*]
3. No unified systems-engineering authority comparable to NASA's Apollo Program Office existed. The Soviet Council of Chief Designers provided coordination but lacked the directive authority of a programme office. After Korolev's death (14 January 1966), no individual held unifying authority across the lunar programme. [Source: Siddiqi; Chertok, *Rockets and People*, NASA SP-4110]
4. Apollo's five-box management structure (NASA Headquarters programme office, three centre directors — MSC, MSFC, KSC — and prime contractors under a single programme manager) provided unified systems-integration authority. The programme achieved a 100% mission safety record and 85.7% landing success rate (6 of 7 landing attempts; Apollo 13 being the exception that safely returned its crew). [Source: CL-APOLLO-005; Appendix 1C]
5. The Soviet programme's early failures (1958–1965) were predominantly launch vehicle failures (12 of 14 missions failed), reflecting immature launcher technology as much as organisational problems. [Source: Appendix 3A; Siddiqi]
6. After Lavochkin assumed sole responsibility for robotic lunar missions (1965 onwards), the success rate improved markedly. The E-8 series (Luna 15–24, 1969–1976) achieved 10 successes in 15 missions (67%), compared to the E-6 series' 3 successes in 12 missions (25%). [Source: Appendix 3A; Harvey]
7. Interface failures between bureaux were documented: the N1 first-stage engine problems stemmed partly from the separation between Kuznetsov's engine bureau (OKB-276) and Korolev/Mishin's integration bureau, with inadequate system-level testing of the 30-engine cluster. [Source: Siddiqi; Chertok]

#### Section D — Comparison

Prediction 1: **Confirmed.** 31% overall Luna success rate versus Apollo's 100% crew safety record and 85.7% landing success rate. The difference is stark, though the programmes are not perfectly comparable (robotic vs. crewed; different risk tolerance).  
Prediction 2: **Confirmed.** Three competing bureaux with no unified programme office.  
Prediction 3: **Partially confirmed.** The N1 engine-integration failure (Prediction 3) is attributable to bureau fragmentation. However, many early failures were launch vehicle failures that owe more to technological immaturity than to inter-bureau coordination.  
Prediction 4: **Confirmed.** Apollo's five-box structure under a single programme manager is associated with high reliability (see CL-APOLLO-005).  
Prediction 5: **Confirmed.** Lavochkin's assumption of sole robotic authority correlated with improved success rates (25% to 67%).

The plausible alternative has substantial merit: technological immaturity and aggressive launch tempo explain much of the early failure rate. Bureau fragmentation is one causal factor among several, not the sole explanation.

#### Section E — Classification

**COMPATIBLE**

The evidence supports the claim that bureau fragmentation reduced reliability, with two important qualifications. First, the causal link is clearest in the N1 engine-integration failures and in the improved reliability observed after Lavochkin consolidated robotic missions; it is weaker for the early programme where launcher immaturity was the dominant failure mode. Second, the comparison with Apollo is structurally sound (the five-box model demonstrably provided integration authority) but involves comparing robotic and crewed programmes with different risk tolerances and testing philosophies. The claim is compatible with the evidence but should not be read as attributing all Soviet failures to organisational structure.

### CL-LUNA-002 — Exploration Firsts

**Entry ID:** CL-LUNA-002  
**Global ID:** CL-2026-026  
**Selection Mode:** REQUEST  
**Domain:** Mission history, programme achievements  
**Claim Type:** H  
**Scope:** Whether the Soviet programme achieved a series of exploration firsts (impact, soft landing, sample return) that Apollo did not prioritise. "Firsts" are defined as the first confirmed achievement of each category by any space programme. The comparison with Apollo concerns programme priorities, not capability.  
**Op. Fidelity:** High — all firsts are well documented in primary sources and uncontested.

#### Section A — Claim

The Soviet programme achieved a series of exploration firsts (impact, soft landing, sample return) that Apollo did not prioritise.

#### Section B — Predictions

If the claim is true, we expect:

1. The Soviet programme achieved documented firsts in multiple categories of lunar exploration.
2. These categories (robotic impact, soft landing, orbiting, sample return, rover deployment) were not primary Apollo objectives.
3. Apollo's objectives were focused on crewed landing, not robotic exploration firsts.
4. The Soviet firsts preceded any comparable Apollo achievement in each category.

#### Section C — Constraints

1. **Luna 2** (12 September 1959): First human-made object to reach the Moon (impact). No prior spacecraft from any programme had achieved lunar contact. [Source: Appendix 3A; NASA historical records]
2. **Luna 3** (4 October 1959): First photographs of the lunar far side. No prior mission had imaged the far side. [Source: Appendix 3A; Siddiqi]
3. **Luna 9** (3 February 1966): First spacecraft to achieve a soft landing on the Moon. Surveyor 1 (NASA) followed on 2 June 1966, four months later. [Source: Appendix 3A; Siddiqi]
4. **Luna 10** (3 April 1966): First spacecraft to orbit the Moon. Lunar Orbiter 1 (NASA) followed on 14 August 1966. [Source: Appendix 3A; Siddiqi]
5. **Luna 16** (24 September 1970): First robotic sample return from the Moon (101 grams). Apollo 11 had returned crewed samples in July 1969, but Luna 16 was the first *robotic* (autonomous) sample return. [Source: Appendix 3A; Harvey]
6. **Luna 17 / Lunokhod 1** (17 November 1970): First successful lunar rover. Operated for 322 days, travelled 10.5 km. No comparable Apollo rover was deployed until the Lunar Roving Vehicle on Apollo 15 (July 1971), which was crewed, not robotic. [Source: Appendix 3A; Harvey]
7. Apollo's stated objective was "landing a man on the Moon and returning him safely to the Earth" (Kennedy, 25 May 1961). Robotic precursor missions (Ranger, Surveyor, Lunar Orbiter) were supporting activities for the crewed programme, not ends in themselves. [Source: CL-APOLLO-001; A-P05]
8. The Soviet programme pursued robotic firsts both as programme objectives in their own right and as substitutes after the crewed landing programme (N1/L3) failed. Luna 16's sample return was partly a response to Apollo 11 — demonstrating that samples could be obtained robotically. [Source: Siddiqi; Harvey]

#### Section D — Comparison

Prediction 1: **Confirmed.** Six documented firsts across distinct categories: impact (Luna 2), far-side photography (Luna 3), soft landing (Luna 9), lunar orbit (Luna 10), robotic sample return (Luna 16), and rover deployment (Luna 17).  
Prediction 2: **Confirmed.** Apollo did not target robotic impact, robotic soft landing, or robotic sample return as programme objectives. Apollo's Ranger, Surveyor, and Lunar Orbiter missions were precursors to the crewed landing, not primary objectives.  
Prediction 3: **Confirmed.** Apollo's sole stated objective was crewed lunar landing and return.  
Prediction 4: **Confirmed.** In every robotic category, the Soviet achievement preceded any comparable NASA achievement: Luna 2 (impact, 1959) preceded Ranger 7 (1964); Luna 9 (soft landing, Feb 1966) preceded Surveyor 1 (Jun 1966); Luna 10 (orbit, Apr 1966) preceded Lunar Orbiter 1 (Aug 1966); Luna 16 (robotic sample return, 1970) has no Apollo equivalent; Luna 17 (robotic rover, 1970) has no Apollo equivalent.

#### Section E — Classification

**COMPATIBLE**

The claim is unambiguously supported. The Soviet programme achieved six documented firsts in lunar exploration, all preceding any comparable achievement by another programme. Apollo did not prioritise these categories — its objective was crewed landing, which it achieved first (Apollo 11, July 1969). The two programmes had fundamentally different exploration strategies: the Soviet programme pursued breadth of robotic firsts; Apollo pursued depth through crewed presence.

#### Section F — Bibliography

- Siddiqi, A. A. *Challenge to Apollo.* NASA SP-2000-4408. 2000.
- Harvey, B. *Soviet and Russian Lunar Exploration.* Springer, 2007.
- Appendix 3A: Luna Programme Mission Manifest.
- CL-APOLLO-001 (Programme objective achievement).

### CL-LUNA-003 — Political Opacity and Error Correction

**Entry ID:** CL-LUNA-003  
**Global ID:** CL-2026-027  
**Selection Mode:** REQUEST  
**Domain:** Organisational behaviour, programme governance  
**Claim Type:** H  
**Scope:** Whether political opacity suppressed effective error correction across programme iterations. "Political opacity" means: classification of failures, absence of published review board reports, suppression of public failure information, and inter-bureau secrecy. "Effective error correction" means: systematic identification of failure causes, implementation of fixes, and non-recurrence of the same failure modes. Comparison set: Apollo's published review board process (see CL-APOLLO-010).  
**Op. Fidelity:** Low-to-moderate — the claim concerns organisational processes that were deliberately obscured; evidence is largely retrospective and reconstructed from post-Soviet archival access.

#### Section A — Claim

Political opacity suppressed effective error correction across programme iterations.

#### Section B — Predictions

If the claim is true, we expect:

1. Failures were not publicly disclosed or were disguised (e.g., under Kosmos designations).
2. No equivalent of Apollo's published review board reports existed for Soviet lunar failures.
3. Inter-bureau competition discouraged information sharing about failures.
4. Similar failure modes recurred across programme iterations.
5. Leadership transitions (particularly Korolev's death) created knowledge gaps that opacity exacerbated.

*Plausible alternative (G-6):* Internal Soviet engineering processes may have been more effective at error correction than the external opacity suggests. The improving success rate across programme phases (25% E-6 to 67% E-8) indicates that *some* learning occurred despite the opacity. The problem may have been concentrated at the political-engineering boundary (hiding failures from leadership and the public) rather than within engineering teams.

#### Section C — Constraints

1. Failed Soviet lunar missions were routinely disguised under Kosmos designations or not announced at all. Of the 48 missions in Appendix 3A, at least 12 were launched under cover designations or without public acknowledgement. Successes were announced; failures were concealed. [Source: Siddiqi; Harvey]
2. No Soviet equivalent of NASA's published review board reports (e.g., Apollo 204 Review Board, Apollo 13 Review Board) has been identified. Internal investigations occurred but reports were classified. Post-Soviet archival access has partially reconstructed some failure analyses. [Source: Siddiqi; Chertok]
3. The competitive bureau structure created institutional barriers to information sharing. Chertok describes cases where Korolev's OKB-1 and Chelomei's OKB-52 withheld technical information from each other. Lavochkin's engineers had limited access to OKB-1's crewed programme data. [Source: Chertok, *Rockets and People*, Vols. 3–4]
4. Korolev died on 14 January 1966. His successor, Vasily Mishin, lacked Korolev's political authority and technical breadth. Chertok and Siddiqi both describe a leadership vacuum that fragmented decision-making further. Mishin's difficulties were compounded by the absence of documented institutional knowledge — much of Korolev's integration authority had been personal, not procedural. [Source: Siddiqi; Chertok]
5. The N1 programme experienced four consecutive first-stage failures (1969–1972). All four failures involved the 30-engine NK-15/NK-33 first stage. The second launch (3 July 1969) destroyed the launch pad. Despite this, no public investigation was conducted, no programme review equivalent to Apollo's post-failure process occurred, and the N1 programme continued with incremental modifications until cancellation in 1974. [Source: Siddiqi; Harvey]
6. Contrast with Apollo: NASA published the Apollo 204 Review Board report (1967), implemented 1,400+ engineering changes, and restructured contractor oversight — all publicly documented. The Apollo 13 Review Board report was similarly transparent. These processes are documented in CL-APOLLO-010. [Source: CL-APOLLO-010; A-P02; A-P03]
7. The improving robotic mission success rate (CL-LUNA-001, Constraint 6) indicates that engineering-level error correction did occur within Lavochkin's consolidated programme, suggesting the plausible alternative has partial validity. [Source: Appendix 3A]

#### Section D — Comparison

Prediction 1: **Confirmed.** Systematic concealment of failures under Kosmos designations or non-announcement.  
Prediction 2: **Confirmed.** No published review board reports. Internal investigations were classified.  
Prediction 3: **Confirmed.** Chertok documents inter-bureau information barriers.  
Prediction 4: **Partially confirmed.** The N1 experienced four consecutive first-stage failures without a corrective process comparable to Apollo's review boards. However, the robotic programme showed improving success rates, indicating some error correction at the engineering level.  
Prediction 5: **Confirmed.** Korolev's death created a documented leadership vacuum. His personal authority had substituted for procedural integration; no institutional process replaced it.

The plausible alternative is partially supported: engineering-level learning occurred within consolidated teams (Lavochkin robotic programme), but the N1/crewed programme — which crossed bureau boundaries and required political-level coordination — showed persistent failure without effective correction.

#### Section E — Classification

**COMPATIBLE**

Political opacity suppressed effective error correction, most clearly in the N1 crewed programme where four consecutive failures proceeded without published review or transparent corrective process. The evidence is strongest at the political-engineering boundary (concealment from public and leadership) and at inter-bureau interfaces (information hoarding). Within consolidated engineering teams (Lavochkin's robotic programme), opacity was less damaging and reliability improved. The claim is compatible with the evidence but should be read as describing a systemic tendency, not an absolute suppression of all learning.

#### Section F — Bibliography

- Siddiqi, A. A. *Challenge to Apollo.* NASA SP-2000-4408. 2000.
- Chertok, B. *Rockets and People.* Vols. 1–4. NASA SP-4110. 2005–2012.
- Harvey, B. *Soviet and Russian Lunar Exploration.* Springer, 2007.
- CL-APOLLO-010 (Post-failure organisational learning).
- A-P02: *Report of Apollo 204 Review Board.* NASA. 1967.
- A-P03: Cortright, E. M. *Report of Apollo 13 Review Board.* NASA. 1970.

### CL-LUNA-004 — Luna 25 Failure and Systemic Continuity

**Entry ID:** CL-LUNA-004  
**Global ID:** CL-2026-028  
**Selection Mode:** REQUEST  
**Domain:** Systems engineering, organisational risk  
**Claim Type:** H  
**Scope:** Whether the Luna 25 failure (2023) reflects continuity of systemic organisational risk from the Soviet era. "Systemic organisational risk" means: institutional factors (supply chain dependencies, quality assurance gaps, loss of institutional knowledge) rather than isolated component failures. The claim posits continuity between Soviet-era and post-Soviet organisational weaknesses, not that the specific failure mechanism was identical.  
**Op. Fidelity:** Moderate — the Luna 25 failure investigation results are public; the organisational continuity argument requires interpretive inference.

#### Section A — Claim

The Luna 25 failure (2023) reflects continuity of systemic organisational risk from the Soviet era.

#### Section B — Predictions

If the claim is true, we expect:

1. The Luna 25 failure was caused by an organisational or systemic factor, not an isolated random component failure.
2. The organisational factor has identifiable parallels in Soviet-era programme weaknesses.
3. The 47-year gap between Luna 24 (1976) and Luna 25 (2023) eroded institutional knowledge, compounding inherited organisational risks.
4. Post-2014 sanctions created supply chain disruptions that interact with pre-existing organisational vulnerabilities.

*Plausible alternative (G-6):* The Luna 25 failure may be better explained as a consequence of the 47-year gap and post-2014 sanctions specifically, rather than continuity of Soviet-era organisational risk. The failure mechanism (domestic component substitution after sanctions) is a post-Soviet phenomenon. Attributing it to "continuity" may overstate the connection to the Soviet programme.

#### Section C — Constraints

1. Luna 25 launched 10 August 2023 on a Soyuz 2.1b from Vostochny Cosmodrome. It was the first Russian lunar mission since Luna 24 (1976). [Source: Appendix 3A; Roscosmos]
2. The failure causal chain, per the Roscosmos investigation: the onboard computer failed to activate the BIUS-L accelerometer unit during a pre-landing orbital correction manoeuvre. With zero angular velocity data, the onboard computer could not regulate the engine burn. The engine fired for 127 seconds instead of the planned 84 seconds. The spacecraft impacted the inner rim of Pontecoulant G crater, approximately 400 km from the intended landing site. [Source: VERIFICATION-HARBOURMASTER.md; Roscosmos investigation report]
3. The BIUS-L accelerometer was a domestically produced replacement for a previously imported component. The substitution was necessitated by post-2014 Western sanctions on technology exports to Russia. The domestic replacement had not undergone equivalent qualification testing. [Source: VERIFICATION-HARBOURMASTER.md; Zak, RussianSpaceWeb]
4. Soviet-era organisational weaknesses relevant to continuity: (a) inadequate system-level testing — the N1 was never ground-tested as a complete system (see CL-LUNA-005); (b) quality assurance gaps at bureau interfaces; (c) reliance on individual expertise rather than procedural knowledge capture. [Source: Siddiqi; Chertok]
5. The 47-year gap between Luna 24 and Luna 25 entailed near-complete loss of the engineering workforce that built and operated the original Luna spacecraft. IKI (Space Research Institute) and NPO Lavochkin retained institutional identity but not operational lunar expertise. [Source: Harvey; Zak]
6. The failure mode — inadequate qualification of a substituted component leading to system-level failure during a critical manoeuvre — echoes Soviet-era patterns of insufficient system integration testing, though the proximate cause (sanctions-driven substitution) is specific to the post-Soviet context. [Source: VERIFICATION-HARBOURMASTER.md]
7. India's Chandrayaan-3 successfully soft-landed on the Moon on 23 August 2023, thirteen days after Luna 25's crash. This contemporaneous comparison suggests the failure was programme-specific, not an inherent difficulty of lunar landing in the modern era. [Source: ISRO; CL-CHANDRA-003]

#### Section D — Comparison

Prediction 1: **Confirmed.** The failure was systemic: inadequate qualification of a domestically substituted component (BIUS-L), not a random hardware failure. The onboard computer software did not include adequate fault handling for BIUS-L activation failure.  
Prediction 2: **Partially confirmed.** The pattern of insufficient system-level testing has Soviet-era precedent (N1 never ground-tested as a system; inter-bureau interface failures). However, the specific mechanism (sanctions-driven domestic substitution) is a post-Soviet phenomenon.  
Prediction 3: **Confirmed.** The 47-year gap eroded institutional knowledge. The engineering workforce turned over completely.  
Prediction 4: **Confirmed.** Post-2014 sanctions forced domestic component substitution (BIUS-L) without equivalent qualification, directly contributing to the failure.

The plausible alternative is partially valid: the proximate cause is post-Soviet (sanctions-driven substitution), not a direct inheritance from the Soviet programme. The continuity argument is strongest at the organisational pattern level (insufficient system testing, quality assurance gaps) rather than the specific mechanism level.

#### Section E — Classification

**COMPATIBLE**

The Luna 25 failure reflects organisational risk patterns — insufficient system-level testing, inadequate component qualification, and weak fault tolerance — that have documented precedent in the Soviet programme. The continuity is at the pattern level, not the mechanism level: the proximate cause (sanctions-driven domestic component substitution) is specific to the post-Soviet context, but the organisational failure to adequately qualify and integration-test the substituted component echoes Soviet-era weaknesses. The 47-year gap and workforce loss amplified inherited vulnerabilities rather than creating entirely new ones.

### CL-LUNA-005 — N1 Failures and Crewed Landing Cancellation

**Entry ID:** CL-LUNA-005  
**Global ID:** CL-2026-029  
**Selection Mode:** REQUEST  
**Domain:** Launch vehicle engineering, programme history  
**Claim Type:** H  
**Scope:** Whether the Soviet decision not to pursue crewed lunar landing was driven by launcher failures (N1), not strategic choice. "Driven by" means the N1 failures were the primary causal factor in the programme's termination. "Strategic choice" would mean a deliberate decision to deprioritise crewed landing independent of N1 performance. The L3 complex (LOK + LK lander) is in scope as the crewed architecture dependent on N1.  
**Op. Fidelity:** High — N1 launch records are well documented; the programme cancellation is a matter of historical record.

#### Section A — Claim

The Soviet decision not to pursue crewed lunar landing was driven by launcher failures (N1), not strategic choice.

#### Section B — Predictions

If the claim is true, we expect:

1. The N1 experienced repeated launch failures that prevented any crewed lunar mission.
2. The L3 crewed lunar complex (LOK orbiter + LK lander) was developed and could have supported a crewed mission if N1 had succeeded.
3. No high-level strategic decision to abandon crewed lunar landing preceded or was independent of the N1 failures.
4. The programme was cancelled because the launcher could not be made to work, not because the mission objective was deprioritised.
5. After Apollo 11 succeeded (July 1969), the political motivation for a Soviet crewed landing diminished — but this alone did not cause cancellation; the N1 failures made continuation technically untenable.

*Plausible alternative (G-6):* The cancellation may have been partly strategic. After Apollo 11 achieved the first crewed landing in July 1969, the political rationale for a Soviet "first" evaporated. Soviet leadership may have chosen to redirect resources to space stations (Salyut) as a domain where they could lead, treating the crewed lunar programme as a lost race rather than a continuing technical objective. The N1 failures provided a convenient justification for a decision that was also politically motivated.

#### Section C — Constraints

1. The N1 (11A52) was the Soviet counterpart to Saturn V, designed to launch the L3 crewed lunar complex. It stood 105 m tall, with a first-stage thrust of approximately 45,400 kN (10,200,000 lbf) from 30 NK-15 engines (later NK-33 in the N1F variant). [Source: Appendix 3B; Siddiqi]
2. Four N1 launches were attempted, all ending in failure during the first-stage burn: [Source: Siddiqi; Harvey]

| Flight | Date | Duration | Failure Mode |
|--------|------|----------|-------------|
| 3L | 21 Feb 1969 | 68.7 s | Engine shutdown due to fire in tail section; all engines commanded off |
| 5L | 3 Jul 1969 | 0.5 s (23 s to ground impact) | Foreign object ingested into oxidiser pump of engine #8; explosion destroyed pad |
| 6L | 27 Jun 1971 | 50.2 s | Unexpected roll moment; guidance unable to compensate; vehicle broke up |
| 7L | 23 Nov 1972 | 106.9 s | Hydraulic shock wave in propellant lines at programmed engine shutdown (6 of 30 engines); first-stage breakup |

3. All four failures occurred during the first-stage burn. No N1 flight progressed to second-stage ignition. The 30-engine first-stage cluster was the persistent point of failure. [Source: Siddiqi]
4. The L3 complex consisted of the LOK (Lunar Orbital Ship — analogous to Apollo CSM) and the LK (Lunar Ship — analogous to Apollo LM, but for a single cosmonaut). The LK was tested in Earth orbit (Kosmos 379, 382, 434 in 1970–1971). The LOK was never tested in space as a complete system. Neither was tested in lunar space. [Source: Siddiqi; Chertok]
5. The N1 programme was officially cancelled by a Central Committee decree in 1974. Vasily Mishin was replaced as head of TsKBEM by Valentin Glushko in May 1974. Glushko immediately terminated the N1 and proposed the Energia launcher as a replacement (which would not fly until 1987). [Source: Siddiqi; Chertok]
6. After Apollo 11 (July 1969), the political urgency of a Soviet crewed lunar landing diminished. The Politburo's interest shifted toward the Salyut space station programme (first launch April 1971), which offered a domain where the Soviet Union could establish primacy. [Source: Siddiqi; Harvey]
7. Siddiqi argues that the cancellation was "overdetermined" — the N1 failures made continuation technically untenable, while the loss of the political race to Apollo made continuation politically unjustifiable. Neither factor alone fully explains the cancellation; together they made it inevitable. [Source: Siddiqi, pp. 837–856]
8. Two completed N1 vehicles (8L, 9L) were on the ground at Baikonur when the programme was cancelled. They were subsequently scrapped. The hardware existed for further tests, but political will and institutional authority did not. [Source: Siddiqi; Harvey]

#### Section D — Comparison

Prediction 1: **Confirmed.** Four consecutive first-stage failures. No N1 flight reached second-stage ignition.  
Prediction 2: **Partially confirmed.** The LK lander was tested in Earth orbit. The LOK was not space-tested. Neither was tested in lunar space. The L3 complex was partially developed but not operationally proven.  
Prediction 3: **Partially disconfirmed.** The political rationale diminished after Apollo 11. The Politburo's shift toward space stations was a strategic choice, not purely a consequence of N1 failures.  
Prediction 4: **Confirmed with qualification.** The N1 failures made continuation technically untenable — but Siddiqi's "overdetermined" framing is more accurate: political and technical factors reinforced each other.  
Prediction 5: **Confirmed.** Apollo 11's success reduced political motivation; N1 failures eliminated technical feasibility. Both factors contributed.

The plausible alternative has significant validity. The cancellation was not a pure technical failure story. It was a convergence of technical failure (N1) and political reassessment (lost race to Apollo, shift to stations). Attributing the cancellation solely to launcher failures understates the political dimension.

#### Section E — Classification

**COMPATIBLE**

The N1 failures were the primary technical driver of the crewed lunar programme's cancellation: four consecutive first-stage failures over three years, with no successful flight, made continuation technically untenable. However, the claim's framing — "driven by launcher failures, not strategic choice" — overstates the case by presenting a false dichotomy. The cancellation was overdetermined: N1 failures eliminated technical feasibility, while Apollo 11's success and the Politburo's strategic shift to space stations eliminated political motivation. The claim is COMPATIBLE with the evidence regarding the N1's causal role, but the "not strategic choice" component is only partially supported. The N1 failures were necessary for cancellation; they were probably not sufficient alone.
